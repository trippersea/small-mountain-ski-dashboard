The Friday-night problem

You’ve got a dozen hills within reach. Some picked up snow; some will be slammed on Saturday; some are on your pass. The usual move is to open too many sites, guess at reports, and hope. Sometimes you burn half a day when better snow was an hour closer.

How we rank options for you

Each mountain gets a fit score from 0–100 that is not a permanent rating. It’s recomputed for your location, weather snapshot, and sliders—the same hill can rank high for you and lower for someone else, or shift after the forecast updates.

Six pieces feed that score:

Snow quality
72-hour forecast snowfall from Open-Meteo, weighted by your snow preference setting. Fresh snow matters more when you set "Powder Day" than when you select "Any Snow."
Skiability
A combination of vertical drop, acreage, trail count, and forecast temperature and wind. High winds and warm temperatures lower this score even if snowfall looks good.
Mountain fit
How well the mountain matches what you said you want for size and how steep or mellow the place tends to feel. Favoring big hills bumps high-vertical spots; favoring local hills does the opposite.
Drive time
Actual road routing via OSRM, with haversine estimates as a fast fallback. Mountains closer to your starting location score higher. Adjusted for your day-trip vs. weekend distance setting.
Value
Ticket price relative to what you get for it. Pass-affiliated resorts score well when you have the relevant pass. High-priced day tickets lose points when cheaper alternatives are available.
Crowd outlook
A predictive model that estimates lift line pressure for a specific mountain on a specific day. It combines population gravity from nearby metros, pass holder density, resort lift capacity, day of week, holiday calendar, and weather demand signals — including whether fresh powder or a bluebird day is likely to spike turnout.

You control how much each factor matters using the preference sliders. Crank snow and drop value for a powder bias; favor small hills and low crowds for quiet locals. The ranking follows your settings—not a one-size formula.

A note on crowd prediction

Most ski sites tell you what conditions are like right now. They report yesterday's snowfall, today's open lifts, and this morning's grooming. None of that tells you whether the parking lot will be full by 8:30am on Saturday.

WhereToSkiNext is the only ski tool that models crowd pressure for a specific mountain on a specific day before you leave home. The crowd score accounts for how many people live within driving distance of that mountain, whether it's on a major pass with millions of holders, what the lift infrastructure can realistically absorb, whether it's a holiday week, and whether a powder day or perfect bluebird conditions will spike demand. It was calibrated so that a resort like Killington correctly scores as Busy on a typical Saturday — not Light-Moderate — and so that a small independent hill scores differently from a large destination resort even when they're the same distance away.

It's a modeled estimate, not a live lift-line camera. But it's meaningfully better than nothing, and nothing else in the consumer ski space is trying to answer that specific question.

Where the numbers come from

Snow forecasts use Open-Meteo (open, model-based data). We request conditions at each mountain’s summit coordinates and refresh when you use the site. The three-day snowfall total is the main input for the snow-quality factor.

Drive times use road routing via OSRM when available; if routing is slow or unavailable, we fall back to distance-based estimates and mark those times with a ~ so you know.

Limits worth knowing:

  • Forecasts can shift—always double-check the morning you head out.
  • We’re not replacing the mountain’s own snow report or on-the-ground patrol calls.
  • Crowd outlook is a predictive model, not a live lift-line count — treat it as a strong signal, not a guarantee.

Independence and sponsors. Rankings are not pay-to-play. Sponsored or featured partners are labeled on the site; they do not change the math that orders your results. A featured mountain still sits in its real scored position in the table—we just make it obvious when something is a partnership placement.

Why I built this

Circa 1999, Bousquet Mountain, Pittsfield MA

I grew up skiing Bousquet in the Berkshires. I raced there through high school and developed a deep love for what a small mountain feels like: no high-speed quads, no resort villages, just skiing. That culture stuck with me.

Now, when friends ask me where they should head for the weekend, I never just give them a name. I think about where they're driving from, if they're bringing kids, and whether they want a mellow day or a big vertical burn. My own family trips work the same way. I want my kids to experience it all, from the small hills with ancient lifts and great waffles to the massive resorts with world-class infrastructure.

I realized that even though you can Google "ski mountains," you still end up doing all the heavy lifting yourself, checking forecasts on one site, pass coverage on another, and guessing at drive times. Most search results just point you toward the same ten famous mountains everyone already knows about.

That's the gap WhereToSkiNext fills. It's not a directory or a static "best-of" list. It's a tool that takes your specific situation and finds the mountain that actually fits. You might end up at a big name like Stowe, or you might end up at an independent hill you've never heard of that turns out to be exactly what you needed.
